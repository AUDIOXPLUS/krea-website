KREA   SIGNATURE SERIES  — 2 —  01 UNDERHUNG MOTOR  90% of high-end loudspeakers on the market use an overhung motor. Long coil, short gap. It has been the dominant topology for decades because it is efficient at converting magnet into sensitivity. But it has a structural problem that no optimization can eliminate: the moment the coil moves, the windings exit the gap. The Bl force factor degrades. Distortion rises. The sound that comes out is no longer what went in. And the greater the excursion, the worse it gets. The remaining 10% — those who chose the underhung topology — solved the linearity problem, but found themselves dealing with everything else. A longer gap means a larger and more expensive magnet. It means a more complex, heavier, harder-to-optimize magnetic circuit. And above all, in the vast majority of cases, it means limited excursion. Because lengthening the gap to keep the coil immersed is costly in terms of flux, weight, and size. So most commercial underhung drivers stop at 5–6 mm of linear excursion. The Bl is flat, yes — but over a stroke too short to compete with the bass response of overhung counterparts.  Not us.  The SIGNATURE S6 motor is underhung with 30 mm of linear excursion. Thirty millimeters in which every single winding of the coil remains completely immersed in the magnetic field. The Bl does not degrade, does not oscillate, does not introduce phantom harmonics. Not at 5 mm. Not at 15 mm. Not at 30 mm. Distortion at full excursion remains below 0.5% THD, where an equivalent overhung exceeds 2–5%. The S3 achieves a remarkable 15 mm figure that has no equal in any other 3" loudspeaker. The difference is not something you read in the specifications. You hear it. Just listen.  03 SPACE-ALLOY MOTOR ASSEMBLY  N55 MAGNET  Here we enter territory where most loudspeaker manufacturers have never set foot. The magnetic circuit of every high-end loudspeaker on the market — without exception — uses mild steel in the parts where magnetic flux travels. The gap walls, the pole, the topplate. Mild steel. A material that saturates at approximately 2.0 Tesla. Beyond that limit, any additional flux generated by the magnet does not pass: it disperses, it is wasted, it never reaches the coil. SIGNATURE does not use mild steel — instead it uses an alloy developed for aerospace and nuclear applications. Magnetic saturation: well beyond the limits of steel. That additional saturation headroom is not a number on a datasheet. It is the space that allows pushing flux into the gap at levels that would cause saturation and performance collapse with any steel. And precisely because this alloy does not saturate where steel would fail, we can use N55 magnets — the highest grade of neodymium commercially available. Any other motor with standard steel attempting to use N55 would see the pole saturate and the excess flux dissipate into nothing. With our alloy, every Tesla generated by the magnet reaches the gap. The motor assembly alone of the S6 has a material cost that exceeds the final retail price of many complete loudspeakers on the market. But it is not an exercise in luxury. It is the only way to achieve the flux density required to guarantee a linear Bl over 30 mm of excursion in a motor that remains compact. And the difference in listening is considerable. It is not an industrial design detail. It is a direct consequence of radial magnetization. When flux travels horizontally through the gap, cylindrical geometry becomes the only geometry that guarantees uniform field distribution across 360°. Any asymmetry — an angle, a thickness variation, a non-homogeneous return path — creates zones where the flux is stronger and zones where it is weaker. The coil, traversing, crosses these non-uniformities and converts them into distortion. The optimization of the circular cross-section was conducted via FEM simulation with successive iterations until every hot-spot and every dead zone in the field was eliminated. The result is a uniform flux plateau across the entire height of the gap, at levels that no standard steel could sustain without saturating. Every curve you see in the SIGNATURE motor has a magnetic reason. It is function, not form. And you can hear it.  Just listen.  05 ZERO GLUE MOTOR  NO MAGNETIC DISCONTINUITIES  In virtually every loudspeaker motor on the market, the components of the magnetic circuit are joined with adhesive. The magnet bonded to the backplate. The topplate bonded to the pole. Sometimes the pole itself is assembled from multiple pieces held together with structural adhesive. It works. But it introduces a problem that the industry has learned to ignore: every adhesive joint is a magnetic discontinuity. Adhesive has a magnetic permeability close to that of air — essentially zero compared to steel or our aerospace alloy. Every bond line between two magnetic components is an invisible gap. A barrier that the flux must cross, losing energy, creating local non-uniformities that propagate through the entire circuit. The result is a field in the gap that is never quite as uniform as the simulation predicted, because the simulation did not model the bondline thicknesses.  The SIGNATURE motor is zero glue.

KREA   SIGNATURE SERIES  — 6 —  06 INTERNAL PHASE PLUG–RADIATOR  Beneath the dust cap of a conventional loudspeaker there is a sealed air chamber. When the cone moves, that air is compressed and decompressed. At low excursions nothing serious happens. At 30 mm of excursion, that air pocket becomes a non-linear pneumatic spring that adds variable stiffness, distortion, and dynamic compression. SIGNATURE integrates an internal phase plug–radiator: a precision component positioned in the pole cavity that linearizes the airflow beneath the dust cap. The air is no longer compressed in a blind space — it is guided, channeled, and vented in a controlled manner. The result is twofold: first, the elimination of non-linear pneumatic compression that corrupts the response at large excursions. Second, an active contribution to thermal dissipation of the coil — the moving air carries heat away from the most critical zone of the loudspeaker. Conventional drivers solve this problem by drilling holes in the pole (pole venting). It works, in a crude way. Our phase plug drills nothing — it shapes the flow. The difference is measured in dB of distortion less and in degrees of coil temperature less under continuous power. It is not a detail visible from the outside. It is not a brochure feature. But it is one of the reasons why SIGNATURE at full excursion sounds as if it were standing still.  Just listen.  07 CARBON FIBER VOICE COIL  DUAL WINDING · EDGE-WOUND FLAT WIRE  The SIGNATURE voice coil former is carbon fiber. Not aluminum, not Kapton, not PEEK. Carbon fiber. Specific stiffness superior to any polymer or lightweight metal, weight lower than aluminum, no eddy currents — unlike metallic formers that interact with the magnetic field and introduce distortion. The conductor wire is flat, wound on edge ("edge-wound"). Not round wire. A round wire in a limited space leaves voids between the windings — air pockets that produce no force, dissipate no heat, and occupy precious space in the magnetic gap. Edge-wound flat wire eliminates the problem at its root: the windings pack one on top of the other with zero gap, like bricks in a perfect wall. The result is the maximum possible number of windings in the magnetic field, the maximum conductor-to-void ratio, and the maximum contact surface between adjacent windings for thermal dissipation.

KREA   SIGNATURE SERIES  — 8 —  08 NO TERMINALS  DIRECT CONNECTION  In the vast majority of loudspeakers, the coil leads are routed to the outside, soldered to metal terminals fixed to the basket, and from there connected to the amplifier via cable. Every junction is a contact point. Every contact point is a parasitic resistance. Every solder joint is a potential future oxidation point. SIGNATURE has no terminals. The coil leads exit directly from the former and are routed to the outside of the loudspeaker without interruption. No intermediate solder joint. No terminal to crimp. No spring connector. This enormously complicates assembly. The mounting procedure becomes a sequence requiring manual attention at every stage — the leads must be guided through the suspension, routed along the basket, and secured without mechanical tension that could damage them during excursion. A single error during assembly and the unit is scrapped. The result is an electrical path from the coil to the external cable that has not a single joint. Zero added parasitic resistance. Zero future oxidation points. Zero microvibrations from loose terminals that after 10 years of use begin to crackle. The contacts are clean and uncontaminated. As they should always be, and as almost no other loudspeaker guarantees. And now premium connection cables truly make sense. Because for the first time, the signal arriving from the coil has not been degraded by joints, solder connections, and terminals before it even reaches the cable. If you invest in quality cables, SIGNATURE is the only driver that guarantees you will not waste that investment. KREA   SIGNATURE SERIES  — 10 —  10 GLASS CRYSTAL CONE  UNIQUE PROFILE · NON-CONVENTIONAL ATTACHMENT  The SIGNATURE cone is made of glass crystal. Not paper, not polypropylene, not aluminum, not kevlar. Glass crystal offers a stiffness-to-mass ratio that conventional materials cannot equal in the same geometry. A cone must do one thing only: move as a rigid piston without flexing. The moment it flexes, it ceases to be a piston and becomes a breakup generator — self-resonances that color the sound with artifacts that were not in the original signal. Paper is light but flexible. Aluminum is rigid but heavy and has a violent, concentrated breakup. Kevlar damps well but has an irregular response. Polypropylene is inexpensive and sounds like it. Glass crystal has high stiffness, contained mass, and a breakup that — when it inevitably arrives at the highest frequencies — is distributed and manageable with the crossover rather than concentrated in a destructive peak. The attachment between cone and coil is non-conventional. It is not the usual cylindrical joint where the neck of the cone slips onto the former. It is a proprietary geometry that optimizes force transfer from the coil to the cone by minimizing stress concentrations and structural resonances of the joint itself. This makes assembly monstrously complicated. The cone profile, the attachment geometry, the required tolerances — everything demands a manufacturing and assembly process that does not forgive errors. But a conventional joint on a glass crystal cone would be the weak point of the entire system. We eliminated the weak point. Every material has a sound. Glass crystal has the sound of transparency.
