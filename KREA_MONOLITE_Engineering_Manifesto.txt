KREA   MONOLITE SERIES  — 3 —  02 UNCONVENTIONAL ARCHITECTURE  PHYSICS WITHOUT SHORTCUTS  The audio industry has spent decades trying to circumvent what physics demands of a true bass system. Ports, passive radiators, transmission lines, bandpass chambers — each promises something for nothing. And each, examined honestly, extracts a price. Ported enclosures borrow bass from resonant port energy. Below the tuning frequency, driver excursion increases with no useful acoustic output. The transducer works hard while producing nothing. The bass that appears around the tuning frequency is, in part, an artifact of stored resonant energy. The timing is different. The transient attack is different. Listeners who have compared both architectures side by side know it. The MONOLITE uses an enclosure architecture that we do not describe in detail. Not to mystify — but because the solution is proprietary and the details are protected. What we can say is this: every note is the direct consequence of transducer motion, not of a resonant auxiliary system adding its own coloration. The rolloff below the operating range is controlled and predictable. The transient response is governed by physics, not by the frequency-selective behavior of a port. The bass you hear from the MONOLITE begins and ends precisely when the recording begins and ends. Nothing is added. Nothing lingers. We did not tune the MONOLITE to impress at a demonstration. We tuned it to be accurate across an entire recording.  Just listen.

KREA   MONOLITE SERIES  — 4 —  03 ZERO TRANSMISSION  THE STRUCTURE DOES NOT PARTICIPATE  Every loudspeaker that moves generates a reaction force. Newton’s third law grants no exceptions to audio manufacturers. In a conventional subwoofer, this force is transmitted into the enclosure, into the surface the enclosure rests on, and from there into the room structure itself. The result is structure-borne vibration: an uncontrolled secondary acoustic source transmitting bass frequencies into walls, floors, shelves, and furniture. It muddies the acoustic image. It creates bass that seems to come from everywhere and nowhere. It is not music. It is interference. The MONOLITE uses an internal mechanical architecture that eliminates net reaction force from the enclosure structure entirely. How this is achieved is proprietary. The result is not. Place the MONOLITE on a glass shelf. On a floating wall panel. On a thin desktop surface. The structure beneath it will not vibrate at bass frequencies. It will not become an unintended acoustic radiator adding colorations that were not in the recording. The bass originates at the MONOLITE membranes, and from nowhere else. This changes what is possible in a listening environment — not only where the subwoofer can be placed, but what the room sounds like when it is playing at full output.  Just listen.

KREA   MONOLITE SERIES  — 5 —  04 34 MM OF LINEAR EXCURSION  PEAK-TO-PEAK · THE ONLY NUMBER THAT PRODUCES BASS  Bass is produced by moving air. The acoustic output of any driver in any enclosure at any frequency is proportional to the product of radiating area and displacement. These are the two variables. Everything else in subwoofer design is commentary. In a compact enclosure, radiating area is constrained. There is no solution to small-enclosure bass reproduction that does not require extraordinary transducer excursion to compensate for limited radiating surface. This is not a design preference. It is a physical necessity. The internal transducer system of the MONOLITE achieves 34 mm of peak-to-peak linear excursion. Conventional subwoofer drivers declare Xmax values of 10 to 15 mm in one direction, representing peak-to-peak displacements of 20 to 30 mm — with linearity that varies significantly across that range. The Xmax figure on most datasheets represents the displacement at which distortion reaches an arbitrary threshold. A substantial portion of the declared travel is already operating in the nonlinear zone. The 34 mm of the MONOLITE is linear travel. Not mechanical travel. Not the threshold of survival. The force factor, suspension compliance, and electrical inductance of the internal system remain within a tightly controlled window across the entire stroke. What goes in, comes out. In a compact enclosure, this is not a specification to be proud of. It is what makes the MONOLITE physically possible.  Just listen.

KREA   MONOLITE SERIES  — 6 —  05 DISTORTION BELOW PERCEPTION  FLAT Bl(x) · FLAT Cms(x) · FLAT Le(x)  Distortion in a subwoofer is not a single number on a datasheet. It is a family of phenomena with different causes, different audible characters, and different engineering solutions. The MONOLITE addresses all of them. The primary source is nonlinearity in the force factor Bl — the variation of the motor’s driving efficiency as the coil moves through its displacement range. A Bl curve that diminishes at the excursion extremes means the amplifier’s signal is translated with varying efficiency at different cone positions. Harmonics are generated that were not in the input. The internal transducer of the MONOLITE presents a Bl curve whose variation across the working range is measured and verified to be minimal — approaching the theoretical ideal of a perfectly uniform field. The second source is suspension nonlinearity: variation in the Cms compliance with displacement. A compliance that stiffens asymmetrically, or progressively with increasing amplitude, generates harmonic and intermodulation products. The Cms curve of the MONOLITE’s internal system is substantially flat across the full working range — not as an approximation, but as a design requirement. The third source — inductance variation Le(x) — modulates the high-frequency motor impedance with cone position, generating intermodulation products between bass content and higher-frequency program material. Its variation in this system is controlled to levels below perceptual significance. The bass you hear from the MONOLITE is the bass that was recorded. Nothing added. Nothing subtracted.  Just listen.

KREA   MONOLITE SERIES  — 8 —  07 THE CABINET  STRUCTURAL INTEGRITY · ZERO STRUCTURAL RADIATION  The walls of a subwoofer enclosure are subject to internal pressure excursions that scale directly with acoustic output. At realistic playback levels at deep bass frequencies, internal pressure variations are substantial. Panel resonances in a conventionally constructed cabinet appear as additive spurious tones — structural notes imposed on the acoustic output that originated in the cabinet walls, not in the recording. The MONOLITE cabinet was designed with each panel treated as an acoustic element requiring its own resonance analysis. Wall geometry, bracing placement, and material damping properties were selected to push all structural resonances outside the operating frequency range of the subwoofer. The result is an enclosure that behaves acoustically as rigid, inert walls — because for the frequencies it reproduces, it is. The compact dimensions of the MONOLITE provide an inherent structural advantage: shorter panel spans mean higher natural resonant frequencies for any given wall thickness. The same panel that would resonate within the bass range in a larger cabinet resonates above the MONOLITE’s operating window. Compactness is not only a space-saving attribute. It is a structural engineering asset. Combined with the zero-transmission internal architecture described in section 03, the practical result is a subwoofer that can be placed on any surface without that surface becoming a participant in the reproduction. No structural vibration. No coloration from the room structure. No bass that seems to come from the floor rather than from the speaker. The MONOLITE plays. The furniture does not.  Just listen.

KREA   MONOLITE SERIES  — 9 —  08 Xmax = Xmech  EVERY MILLIMETER PRODUCES MUSIC  The loudspeaker industry has developed a convention worth examining. Manufacturers declare an Xmax — maximum linear excursion — representing the displacement at which some distortion threshold is reached. They separately declare an Xmech — mechanical travel limit before physical damage — which is always a larger number. The gap between them is the zone in which the driver operates beyond its linear range but below the point of destruction. This gap exists because it is commercially convenient. A driver with 8 mm of true linear Xmax and 14 mm of Xmech can be marketed with a 14 mm excursion figure. The distinction is buried in fine print, if it appears at all. In the MONOLITE’s internal transducer system, the measured displacement limits from large-signal analysis confirm that the protection threshold coincides with the point at which the force factor begins to depart from its linear operating range. There is no gray zone. No region of technically-operating-but-already-distorting travel. No spare millimeters dedicated to survivability rather than music. Every millimeter of the MONOLITE’s excursion produces linear acoustic output. Not approximately linear. Linear. When other products declare impressive excursion figures and note in smaller text that this includes the non-linear travel zone, they are confirming that a portion of their claimed stroke is producing distortion, not music. We have no such travel. We have no spare millimeters.   Every millimeter is music.  Just listen.
